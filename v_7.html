✦   Once Upon a Time   ✦

In a quiet little village,
words had time to breathe.

Once upon a time, in a village full of people who had very important things to say to each other, there was a little post office at the end of the lane. And above that post office, in a cosy hollow in the biggest oak tree, lived an owl named Ollie.

Ollie was the Slowmail delivery owl. He had very large eyes, very soft feathers, and one very important rule: every letter he collected would fly for exactly 24 hours before it arrived.

"But why?" the villagers would ask. And Ollie would blink his enormous eyes and say, very wisely: "Because words, like warm soup, taste better when they've had time to settle."

Chapter I

Every evening,
Ollie collected the letters.

At sunset, when the sky turned the colour of peaches and the fireflies came out, Ollie would glide down from his oak tree to the big red post box in the town square.

He would carefully, gently, tuck every single letter under his left wing. Love letters. Thank-you notes. Apologies. Stories. Recipes. Little drawings from children. Big worries from grown-ups. All of them, every shape, every size.

"I'll take good care of these," he would whisper. And then he'd take a big, deep breath — and leap into the darkening sky.

Chapter II

All night long,
Ollie flew.

Past the sleeping chimneys. Past the quiet fields. Past the mountain where the echo lived and the river where the frogs sang in summer. Ollie flew for exactly 24 hours — not one minute more, not one minute less.

He never got tired because he was an owl, and owls are made for the night. And besides — he knew that the letters needed the journey. The flying was part of what made them special.

If you could follow Ollie through the dark, you'd see your words glowing softly under his wing, warm as a lantern. Still you. Still true. Just given time to mean what they meant.

Chapter III

And just as the sun
came up — it arrived.

Exactly 24 hours after Ollie had collected your letter, he would land — very softly, so as not to wake anyone — and pop it through the door.

And the person who found it would hold it in both hands and think: Someone wrote this for me. Yesterday. And kept thinking about me the whole time it was on its way.

That's the magic of Slowmail. Not the flying. Not the waiting. The knowing that someone chose to be patient for you.
