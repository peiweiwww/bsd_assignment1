New Email Service Forces Users to Wait a Full Day Before Messages Are Delivered

Slowmail, a San Francisco start-up, has launched what it calls "the most significant rethinking of electronic mail since its invention" — a platform that holds every message for exactly twenty-four hours before sending it.

By Alexandra Morse  ·  Technology Correspondent  ·  March 28, 2026

In an era defined by the expectation of instantaneous reply, a small company operating from a converted warehouse in San Francisco's Mission district has introduced a product that does the opposite of everything the industry has optimised for: it makes you wait.

Slowmail, founded in 2024, offers a fully functional email client with a single, non-negotiable constraint built into its architecture. Every message composed within the application is held in an encrypted queue for precisely twenty-four hours before it is transmitted to its recipient.

"We are not fixing email," said the company's founder in a brief telephone interview conducted — fittingly — by letter, the reply of which arrived the following morning. "We are restoring to it something that was always there and that we have, through haste, discarded."

"The twenty-four hour delay is not a bug we forgot to fix. It is the entire product."

— Slowmail Founder, via correspondence

The company declined to identify its founder by name, communicating all press inquiries through a postal address. Response times, a spokesperson noted, should be expected within one business day.

24h
An outgoing message as it appears in the Slowmail queue. Users may recall the message at any point during the hold period. (Illustration: T. Hollingsworth)

Early users, numbering some twelve thousand since the service's public beta launched in January, report a phenomenon the company has begun calling "the overnight edit" — a tendency to recall and substantially revise messages after sleeping on them.

"I recalled four emails in the first two weeks," said one beta user, a partnerships manager at a technology company who asked not to be named. "Two of them would have been genuinely damaging. The other two were simply embarrassing. The service has paid for itself many times over."

The service is free to use, with a paid tier available for teams requiring additional storage and read receipts. No feature, the company emphasises, will ever allow a message to bypass the twenty-four hour queue.

Opinion & Commentary
Essay

We Did Not Lose Our Patience. We Were Trained Out of It.

By Constance Halloway  ·  Contributing Writer

The assumption buried in every email client ever built is that speed is virtue. That to reply swiftly is to respect the sender. That to let a message sit unread past lunchtime is a small act of cruelty.

"We were not born in a hurry. We were taught to hurry by tools that profited from our hurrying."

Slowmail does not argue against communication. It argues, quietly and by design, that communication improved by a single night's reflection is communication worth having. The twenty-four hour delay is not an obstacle. It is the editorial process that electronic mail was never given.

Constance Halloway is the author of The Patience Economy.

Counterpoint

A Reasonable Person's Case Against Waiting

By R. Drummond

There are emails that cannot wait twenty-four hours. There are contexts in which delay is not a virtue but a dereliction. Slowmail knows this. Its answer — Instant Mode, opt-in, and deliberately buried in settings — reveals that even its founders understand the limits of their thesis.
