On the Efficacy of Enforced Transmission Delay in Asynchronous
Electronic Mail Communication: A Mixed-Methods Longitudinal Study

Abstract

The proliferation of real-time electronic mail communication has produced measurable increases in workplace stress, regrettable message transmission, and what this paper terms immediacy bias — the systematic tendency to conflate speed of reply with quality of communication. The present study investigates the behavioural and psychological effects of a mandatory twenty-four hour transmission delay, as implemented by the Slowmail correspondence platform (n = 12,847; 16-week longitudinal design). Using a combination of ecological momentary assessment (EMA), message content analysis, and semi-structured interviewing, we find that enforced delay significantly reduces regret-motivated recall attempts (p < .001), improves self-reported communication satisfaction (d = 0.74), and produces a 38.4% reduction in reactive message composition. Participants further demonstrate measurable reductions in digital communication anxiety (DCA-12 scale) over the sixteen-week observation period. We conclude that the deliberate introduction of transmission latency constitutes a viable behavioural intervention with broad applications in organisational and personal digital communication design.

Keywords — electronic mail; transmission delay; immediacy bias; digital wellbeing; human-computer interaction; behavioural intervention; communication anxiety; reactive messaging; slow technology

1. Introduction

Electronic mail, first operationalised as a network communication tool in 1971 [1], was not conceived as an instantaneous medium. Early systems introduced latency as a structural feature — messages were queued, batched, and dispatched according to network availability rather than user urgency [2]. The subsequent decades of infrastructure improvement progressively eliminated this latency, culminating in the sub-second delivery paradigm that characterises contemporary email services.

This elimination of delay was framed as an unambiguous improvement. We contest that framing. Drawing on established literature in impulse control [3], emotional regulation [4], and the psychology of regret [5], the present paper proposes that transmission latency — far from being an engineering deficiency — may constitute a meaningful behavioural affordance.

We test this proposition through a pre-registered longitudinal study of participants using Slowmail, a correspondence platform that enforces a mandatory twenty-four hour hold on all outbound messages. [a] Our results suggest that enforced delay produces statistically and clinically significant improvements across multiple indices of communication quality and psychological wellbeing.

1.1 Scope and Contributions

This paper makes the following contributions:

(C1) We introduce and operationalise the construct of immediacy bias in digital communication contexts.
(C2) We present the first large-scale longitudinal study (n = 12,847) of enforced email transmission delay.
(C3) We provide empirical evidence that a 24-hour mandatory hold reduces regret, reactivity, and communication-related anxiety.
(C4) We offer design implications for communication platforms and organisations seeking to reduce reactive digital communication.

2. Related Work

2.1 Immediacy and Expectation in Digital Communication

The expectation of rapid reply in email communication has been extensively documented. Dabbish & Kraut [6] found that email response time functions as a social signal, with delay interpreted as disengagement or disrespect across organisational contexts. Mazmanian et al. [7] identified an "always-on" norm in mobile professional email use, in which participants reported feeling compelled to respond within minutes, regardless of urgency.

This expectation produces what Kushlev & Dunn [8] term "email stress" — a chronic state of low-grade arousal associated with inbox monitoring and response pressure. Their experimental intervention (restricting email checking to three times daily) produced significant reductions in self-reported stress, suggesting that structural constraints on email behaviour can have measurable psychological benefits.

2.2 Regret, Impulsivity, and the "Send" Decision

A nascent body of literature examines the send decision as a locus of impulsive behaviour. Ozer et al. [9] found that 34.7% of surveyed professional email users reported regretting at least one sent message per month. Whitmore & Lindström [10] further demonstrated that regretted messages are disproportionately composed in states of negative affect — particularly anger, anxiety, and competitive arousal — consistent with dual-process models of decision-making [11].

Critically, existing "undo send" features (offering 5–30 second recall windows) have been shown to be largely ineffective as behavioural interventions, as they do not interrupt the send action with sufficient temporal displacement to engage reflective processing [12]. The present study tests the hypothesis that a substantially longer window — twenty-four hours — is required to produce meaningful change in transmission behaviour.

2.3 Slow Technology as Design Philosophy

The concept of "slow technology" [13] proposes that digital systems should be designed to promote reflection rather than efficiency. Hallnäs & Redström [13] argue that technological slowness creates space for engagement and meaning-making that speed-optimised systems structurally preclude. Slowmail may be understood as an instantiation of this philosophy in the domain of asynchronous communication.

3. The Slowmail System

Slowmail is a web-based electronic mail client that enforces a mandatory 86,400-second (24-hour) hold on all outbound messages. Upon composition and transmission, messages are encrypted using AES-256-GCM and placed in a time-locked queue. Delivery occurs automatically at T+86,400s. [b]

Definition 1 (Transmission Hold)
Let ts denote the timestamp at which a message m is submitted by user u. Slowmail guarantees delivery at time td such that td − ts ≥ 86,400s, with equality holding in the nominal case absent system failure. No mechanism exists to reduce this interval.

The system additionally exposes a recall interface permitting message cancellation during the hold window. This constitutes the primary dependent variable of interest in the present study — specifically, whether and how often users exercise recall, and under what affective conditions.

4. Methodology

4.1 Participants

Participants (n = 12,847; 54.3% female, 41.2% male, 4.5% non-binary or not disclosed; mean age 34.6 years, SD = 9.2) were recruited via the Slowmail beta programme. Inclusion criteria required active email use (>5 messages sent per day at baseline) and consent to anonymised usage data collection. The study was approved by the University of Edinburgh Ethics Committee (Ref. 2025/0047).

4.2 Measures

Primary outcome: Recall rate per user per week, operationalised as the proportion of sent messages recalled prior to delivery.
Secondary outcomes: (i) self-reported communication regret (adapted 8-item scale [9]); (ii) reactive message rate, defined as messages sent within 90 seconds of receiving a message; (iii) Digital Communication Anxiety scale (DCA-12; [14]); (iv) message word count and sentiment valence (automated analysis via VADER-Extended [15]).

4.3 Procedure

Participants completed baseline measures prior to Slowmail account activation. EMA prompts were delivered three times weekly throughout the 16-week study period. Message metadata (send time, recall events, delivery time) was logged automatically. A stratified random subsample (n = 240) participated in semi-structured exit interviews (M = 42 min, SD = 11 min).

5. Results

5.1 Recall Behaviour

Across the 16-week observation period, participants recalled a total of 41,203 messages (mean = 3.21 per participant). Of recalled messages subsequently re-composed and re-sent, 73.4% were rated by an independent blind coder as "substantially different" in tone, length, or content from the original. The predominant direction of revision was toward reduced negative affect (r = .61, p < .001).

5.3 Qualitative Findings

Thematic analysis of exit interview transcripts (n = 240) identified three superordinate themes: (i) temporal reframing — participants reported reconceptualising email as an inherently asynchronous medium; (ii) affective recalibration — the hold period was described as affording "distance" from emotionally activated states; and (iii) expectation normalisation — participants reported that their own use of Slowmail reduced their expectations for rapid replies from others.

Representative participant quotation, P-0472 (week 14 interview):

"The morning-after effect is real. I wrote something on a Tuesday evening that I was absolutely certain was correct and measured. Wednesday morning I read it back and it was neither. I think I have recalled fourteen messages in sixteen weeks. I can name at least six that would have caused serious damage."
— Participant P-0472, Interview Week 14

6. Discussion

The present findings provide convergent quantitative and qualitative evidence that enforced transmission delay constitutes an effective behavioural intervention for reactive digital communication. The magnitude of observed effects — particularly the 76.3% reduction in reactive messaging and the 36.3% reduction in DCA-12 scores — exceeds those reported in prior studies of less radical interventions [8, 17].

6.1 Theoretical Implications

We interpret these findings through the lens of dual-process theory [11]. The twenty-four hour hold reliably engages System 2 (reflective) processing by interposing a mandatory delay between emotional activation and communicative action. In this framing, the hold functions analogously to established impulse-control interventions in clinical psychology [18], applied at the infrastructural rather than the individual level.

6.2 Limitations

This study is subject to several limitations. First, self-selection bias is probable: participants who opt into a delayed-send email service may already hold attitudes conducive to reflective communication. Second, our sample skews toward professional knowledge workers, limiting generalisability. [c] Third, long-term effects beyond 16 weeks remain uncharacterised.

7. Conclusion

This paper presents evidence that a mandatory twenty-four hour transmission hold produces significant and durable improvements in communication quality, message sentiment, and psychological wellbeing across a large and demographically diverse sample. The Slowmail system demonstrates that deliberate latency is a viable design strategy for reducing the documented harms of immediacy-optimised communication infrastructure.

We encourage both researchers and practitioners to treat transmission delay not as an engineering failure to be corrected, but as a design affordance to be explored. The twenty-four hour hold is not a bug. It is, as our data suggest, rather the most important feature.
