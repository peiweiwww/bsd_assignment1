The interval
24hours

One day. Not an inconvenience — a gift. The same span of time that separates a hasty word from a chosen one. A reaction from a response. A message sent in haste from one sent with care.

What happens

Hour 0  ·  You write

Compose your message at whatever hour feels right. The words are yours. Take the time they deserve.

Hours 1–23  ·  We hold

Your letter rests with us — sealed, unread, waiting. This is the interval. The space between intention and consequence.

Hour 24  ·  It arrives

Quietly, exactly on time, your message reaches its reader. Unchanged. Unhurried. Entirely as you meant it.

On slowness
間 Ma

In Japanese aesthetics, ma is the deliberate pause — the silence between notes, the empty space in a room, the breath before a word. Slowmail is built on this idea. The gap between writing and sending is not empty. It is full of meaning.

待 Matsu

Matsu means to wait — but in its fullest sense, to wait with care and readiness. Not passive. Not anxious. A held breath. We ask both writer and reader to practice matsu: one in the composing, one in the receiving.

心 Kokoro

Kokoro is heart, mind, and spirit at once. The 24-hour interval creates space for kokoro to inform your words — to ask whether what you have written is truly what you feel, rather than what you felt in the moment of writing.

形 Katachi

Form is not a constraint. Form is what gives meaning its shape. The single rule of Slowmail — one day, always — is a form. Within that form, your correspondence becomes something different: considered, intentional, and in some small way, beautiful.
